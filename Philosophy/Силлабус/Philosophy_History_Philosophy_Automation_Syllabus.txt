1. Course Description and Goals
      ТAutomationУ is likely one of the first descriptors we would arrive at in trying to define what makes this era contemporary. And yet automation is not a new concept: a technical fascination with automation began in the Renaissance, and a general cultural interest in automata is indexed in literature as early as the 18th century. Students taking this course will explore where the types of language and systems of thinking we use to historicize and theorize automation today come from and how they've been adapted to the contemporary moment. There is a time lag: the discourse which we use to understand contemporary automative technologies is often as old as thinking on automation itself. Likewise, automative technologies are not only historical objects to which we can apply philosophy. As early as Descartes, philosophers have borrowed the imagery and structure of technical novelties as explanatory devices for philosophical problems that are not specifically about automation: philosophy is applied to automative technologies as much as automative technologies are applied to philosophy.
      Through course content on the history and philosophy of automation, students taking this course will be taught to mobilize key scholarly paradigms in the fields of media studies, the history of science, and the philosophy of technology. We will use methodologies from these three fields in order to intervene on three signal problems relevant to automation today. (1) Historiography: when we are telling the story of the development of automative technologies, are we telling a story about technical progress? Should automation be understood as the ideal endpoint of all technological development? (2) The Machine and The Organism: is automation best understood as the progressive replacement of formerly organic functions with technical functions? For example, when we a hydraulic ТarmУ capable of lifting more weight than a human armС have we simply replaced a formerly human function with a better technical function? (3) Labour: For the last 200 years, economic thinking has dominated not only the decisionmaking authority dictating which domains should or should not be automated but the authority behind the description of automation itself. For example, contemporary technologies are often categorized as Тlabour-savingУ or Тlabour-replacing,У which says more about their economic status than it does about their technical properties. Students that successfully complete the course will leave with a historically informed understanding of the technological development of automation, philosophical frameworks with which to understand the impact of historical and contemporary automative technologies, as well as a general understanding of how the fields within the humanities have treated the novel social, political, and philosophical problems posed by automation from the 18th century onwards.
The course will be organized into three 1.5 day units, with each unit based around one of the above themes. The last day of the course will be dedicated to synthesizing these problems and student presentations.
2. Learning Outcomes
Students who successfully complete this course will be able to:
Ґ Distinguish and interpret various histories of automation through key historiographical paradigms of technological development, invention, and innovation.
Ґ Define and utilize multiple operative definitions of automation and their criteria: e.g. technical makeup (structuralism), functionalism (cybernetics), and output (blackboxing).
Ґ Evaluate and mobilize various philosophical treatments of automative technologies as objects of study: e.g. are these technologies tools, systems, or environments?
Ґ Understand how discourse about automation as well as cultural concerns subtending automative development have grown and changed between the 18th and 21st centuries.
Ґ Understand the legacy of automative technologies on philosophical thought through the historicization of technical schemata, metaphors, and models used in philosophical discourse.

3. Course content

Course main topics:

* Introduction: Why Should We Care About the History of Automation?
* Historiography of Technology: Is Technical Development Automative?  ? Machine and Organism: Analogy, Modeling, and Metaphor
* Labour: What Does It Mean to Say That Technology Works?

1. Introduction: Why Should We Care About the History of Automation?

An introduction to the three course problems and the disciplines of media studies, history of science, and the philosophy of technology. We will begin with contemporary concerns and ways of conceptualizing automation in the 21st century and use historical cases to reconstruct where these ideas have come from.

2. Historiography of Technology: Is Technical Development Automative?
An overview of theories of technological development. Here we will focus on the work of Gilbert Simondon, Bertrand Gille, and Donald MacKenzie. We will discuss whether automative development can be understood as progressive development: do technologies get better when they become more automative?

3. Machine and Organism: Analogy, Modeling, and Metaphor
Using cybernetic thinking as a central case, here we will explore what, precisely, is being automated. Do technologies simply replace and augment organic functions (movement, thinking, seeing, etc.) that existed prior to them? Do they radically reshape what we mean when we talk about these functions? Are automative technologies designed on the basis of organic referents? We will attempt to answer these questions using the work of historians of science such as Peter Galison, Georges Canguilhem, and N. Katherine Hayles.

4. Labour: What Does It Mean to Say That Technology Works?

Here we will look at how economic thinking has come to dominate our understanding of technical progress and technological development in the last two centuries. Today, it is almost impossible to talk about automation without talking about human labour. Are these two domains necessarily connected? Was labour the primary domain of concern in early discourses of automation? Can we imagine a metric for the development of automative technologies that is not based on labour? Here we will turn to thinkers such as Aaron Benanav, John May, and Karl Marx, who take up automation in an explicitly economic valence.

4. Types of coursework
During the course students work individually and in groups.

* Group work

Students will be put into groups for studying and responding to materials read for class and lectures given. Alternating roles in facilitating discussion will be assigned to students within these groups. Students will be responsible for daily presentations in front of their classmates that summarize the course of their discussions and the conclusions reached.

* Individual work

Individual preparation for this course is crucial. The seminar format provides the opportunity to arrive at new ideas and go Тoff-script.У This is only possible if each student has a unique reception of the texts read and does not rely on the summaries of others.

* Seminars

Classes will be run in a seminar format, interspersed with a series of short lectures each day. Students will also have the opportunity to engage in groupwork between daily seminars. Please take proper notes, make bullet points, use schemes, and use any creative method that helps you discuss your views and analyses with other groups and the professor.

5. ASSIGNMENTS and EVALUATION
During the course students will be evaluated using a 10-point system. The final grade consists of the following components:  In-class ExamsС 60% of final grade
Students will complete three in-class exams on days 2, 4, and 6 of the course. These exams will consist of a series of short answer questions covering both seminar discussions and texts read. Each of these short answer questions must contain a thesis and an evidence-based (here reading-based) argument that supports the thesis in question. Each answer should be approximately one large paragraph, or 200 words in length. Tests will consist of approximately 6 questions, which can be answered over a 1.5hour period.
Group presentationС20% of final grade
Students will make a presentation in groups during the final two timeslots of the final day of the course. Each group will have 10 minutes time for presentations followed by 10-15 minutes of discussion and commentary. This argument-based (rather than expository) presentation must take a particular automative technology as a case study and mobilize at least two of the methodologies explored during the course in order to support an argument. Presentations will be graded based on the following criteria: (1) clear argumentation; (2)  depth of analysis; (3) originality of the thesis; (4) the ability to answer questions related to the thesis and case study. Students must submit a plan or transcript of their presentations and a short explanation of how each group member contributed to the final project. These explanations of contribution must be consensus based: all group members must agree that the description of each member's contribution is accurately represented.
Class participationС20% of final grade
Participation in a seminar course is critical for creating a positive learning environment as well as comprehending the material. It is especially vital for success in one's education, as we tend to learn more as we engage more. To this end, students will be expected to take an active part in class meetings. Please note that speaking in class does not in itself count towards the participation grade. Successful, graded participation in this course implies that student contributions: 1) demonstrate evidence that the student carefully read the assigned material 2) demonstrate evidence that the student can engage with the material assigned and discuss it with their peers.

Examination
The examination will consist of a 90-minute written test that includes the identification of ten quotations from required course readings and a written essay. The use of any electronic devices is prohibited. The student must:
1) Attribute the given quotations; identify the sections of the texts (such as a chapter) where the quotations are taken from as well as their authors, titles, and year of publication.
2) Based on the given quotations, write an essay in English (1000 words) analyzing semantic relationships between the texts where these quotations are taken from.
