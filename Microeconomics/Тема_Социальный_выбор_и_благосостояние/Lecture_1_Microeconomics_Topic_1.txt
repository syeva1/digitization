=== Slide 03 ===
What Are Preferences?
Preferences describe how an individual ranks alternatives.
Notation: a ≽ b means “a is weakly preferred to b.”
Completeness: any two alternatives can be compared.
Transitivity: rankings are consistent (no cycles).
With completeness & transitivity, preferences can be represented by a utility function.

=== Slide 04 ===
Practice Question 1
Q1. Define completeness and transitivity in preferences.
Why are they important for rational choice?
Page 4 / 20

=== Slide 05 ===
Answer 1
Completeness:
For any two alternatives a, b, either a ≽ b or b ≽ a (or both ⇒ a ∼ b).
Transitivity:
If a ≽ b and b ≽ c, then a ≽ c.
These properties ensure coherent, predictable choices (no preference cycles).

=== Slide 06 ===
From Individuals to Society
A social choice rule combines individual rankings into a social ranking.
Examples:
• Majority rule
• Borda count
• (Extreme) dictatorship
Issue: heterogeneous preferences can yield cycles/inconsistencies.

=== Slide 07 ===
Practice Question 2 — Majority Rule with 3 Voters
Three voters rank Pizza (P), Salad (S), Burger (B):

Voter 1:
1st: P
2nd: S
3rd: B

Voter 2:
1st: S
2nd: B
3rd: P

Voter 3:
1st: B
2nd: P
3rd: S

Using majority rule, determine the winner for:
• P vs S
• S vs B
• B vs P

What do you conclude?
Page 7 / 20

=== Slide 08 ===
Answer 2
P vs S:
Voters 1 & 3 prefer P → P wins (2–1).
S vs B:
Voters 1 & 2 prefer S → S wins (2–1).
B vs P:
Voters 2 & 3 prefer B → B wins (2–1).

Conclusion:
P ≻ S, S ≻ B, B ≻ P — a Condorcet cycle.
Society’s preferences can be intransitive even if each individual is rational.

=== Slide 09 ===
Arrow’s Impossibility Theorem (Informal)
No aggregation rule satisfies all at once:
• Unrestricted domain (any rational profiles allowed)
• Pareto efficiency
• Independence of irrelevant alternatives (IIA)
• Non-dictatorship

Implication:
Every reasonable rule sacrifices at least one desideratum.

=== Slide 10 ===
Measuring Social Welfare
Utilitarian social welfare function (SWF):
W = Σ ui  (maximize total utility)

Rawlsian (Max–Min) SWF:
W = min ui  (focus on the worst-off)

Pareto efficiency:
No feasible change can make someone better off without making someone else worse off.

Efficiency vs equity trade-offs are central.

=== Slide 11 ===
Practice Question 3 — Welfare Comparison
Income allocations ($):

Allocation A:
Alice: 100
Bob: 20

Allocation B:
Alice: 70
Bob: 70

Questions:
• Which allocation does utilitarian welfare prefer?
• Which does Rawlsian welfare prefer?
Page 11 / 20

=== Slide 12 ===
Answer 3
Utilitarian totals:
A = 120
B = 140
→ Utilitarian prefers B.

Rawlsian minima:
min(A) = 20
min(B) = 70
→ Rawlsian prefers B.

Both criteria select B, but for different reasons (sum vs worst-off).

=== Slide 13 ===
Practice Question 4 — Adding a Third Allocation
Add allocation C:

Allocation A:
Alice: 100
Bob: 20

Allocation B:
Alice: 70
Bob: 70

Allocation C:
Alice: 90
Bob: 90

Rank A, B, C under:
• Utilitarian welfare
• Rawlsian welfare
Page 13 / 20

=== Slide 14 ===
Answer 4
Utilitarian sums:
A = 120
B = 140
C = 180
→ Ranking: C ≻ B ≻ A.

Rawlsian minima:
min(A) = 20
min(B) = 70
min(C) = 90
→ Ranking: C ≻ B ≻ A.

Both agree here: C dominates.
In general, the two criteria can diverge.

Practice Question 5 — When Criteria Diverge
Consider:

Allocation D:
Alice: 100
Bob: 100
Carol: 0

Allocation E:
Alice: 60
Bob: 60
Carol: 60

Questions:
• Which allocation is preferred by utilitarian welfare?
• Which by Rawlsian welfare?
Page 15 / 20

=== Slide 16 ===
Answer 5
Utilitarian totals:
D = 200
E = 180
→ Utilitarian prefers D.

Rawlsian minima:
min(D) = 0
min(E) = 60
→ Rawlsian prefers E.

Here the criteria diverge:
Utilitarian favors higher total.
Rawlsian protects the worst-off.

=== Slide 17 ===
Practice Question 6 — Pareto Efficiency
Allocations:

Allocation F:
Alice: 80
Bob: 60

Allocation G:
Alice: 85
Bob: 55

Question:
Are the allocations Pareto comparable?
Is either allocation Pareto dominated?
Page 17 / 20

=== Slide 18 ===
Answer 6
G makes Alice better off (85 vs 80),
but Bob worse off (55 vs 60).

Neither F nor G Pareto dominates the other.
Both are Pareto efficient relative to one another.

=== Slide 19 ===
Key Takeaways
• Individual rationality requires completeness and transitivity.
• Aggregation can generate cycles (Condorcet).
• Arrow shows no perfect aggregation rule.
• Welfare criteria: utilitarian vs Rawlsian.
• Pareto efficiency and equity–efficiency trade-offs.
• Different criteria can agree or diverge depending on distributions.
