1. Course description

Microeconomics considers the behavior of decision-makers within the economy, such as individuals, households, and firms. These behaviors are crucial building blocks of the economy to even understand “macro level” (country or global level) issues; as a result, modern macroeconomics is also micro-founded. Thus, microeconomics is the main tool to understand all aspects of economy, whether it’s at the micro level (decisions of consumers and firms) or macro level (decisions of central banks and governments).

Topics include social choice and welfare, market economy, imperfect competition, imperfect information, communication, and informal markets (social networks).

To explore these topics, students will engage in activities (inside and outside the classroom) to ensure an understanding of the course topics and the applications of such concepts.

2. Course goals

This course will help students to “think like an economist”: they will learn to formulate and analyze models that can provide useful predictions about the economy (and society in general).

3. Learning outcomes

Students who successfully complete this course will be able to:
• understand different types of welfare perspectives, including efficiency and equity.
• understand different types of markets, from free-market to oligopoly and monopoly, as well as informal markets (social networks).
• comprehend the value of information and difficulties associated with asymmetric information (principal-agent problems).
• most importantly, they will be able to develop models about any economic phenomenon and provide valuable predictions about them.

5. Types of coursework

During the course students work individually and in groups.

Group work:
• Students are divided into groups on the first day.
• At the end of each class, each group is assigned a task.
• On the next class, one member from each group is randomly selected to present the group task.

Note:
Group tasks involve standard problem sets as well as more advanced analysis.

Individual work:
• Each student develops an original model about an economic phenomenon.
• Students provide predictions and present their model and results at the end of the course.

6. Assignments and evaluation

The course uses a 10-point grading system.

Final grade components:

Grades from 10 to 5 are considered “pass”.
Grades from 4 to 1 are considered “fail”.
Students who fail must take an examination.

Grading procedure:
If a student fails to complete course components worth 30% or more of the interim grade for valid reasons, the student may be assigned an individual task by the instructor.

Additional rules:
• Violations of academic or corporate ethics may lead to removal from class and grade reduction.
• Late submissions are not accepted and receive 0 points.

Students with insufficient points will have an academic deficiency and must complete a retake session.
During retakes, all components except class participation may be reassessed.

Grades publication:
Grades are published on Canvas.
Students may request feedback individually during the course.
