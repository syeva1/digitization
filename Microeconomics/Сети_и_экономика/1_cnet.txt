=== Slide 03 ===
What is a Market?
A market is any arrangement that enables buyers and sellers to exchange goods, services, or assets.

Key elements:
• participants
• objects of trade
• rules
• prices (not always)
• institutions

Outcomes of interest:
• allocation
• prices
• efficiency
• surplus
• fairness

Market forms range from perfect competition to monopoly/oligopoly, and include matching markets with no prices.

=== Slide 04 ===
Competitive Markets: Intuition
• Many small buyers and sellers, each price-taking.
• Goods are homogeneous; information is complete.
• No externalities; free entry/exit; well-defined property rights.
• Supply–demand interaction yields an equilibrium price p* where QS(p*) = QD(p*).

=== Slide 05 ===
Adam Smith’s Invisible Hand
Core idea:
Individual self-interest, under the right market institutions, can lead to socially desirable outcomes as if guided by an “invisible hand.”

• Each firm maximizes profit.
• Each consumer maximizes utility.
• In competitive equilibrium, no agent can do better given prices.
• Under appropriate assumptions, the equilibrium allocation is Pareto efficient.

=== Slide 07 ===
First Welfare Theorem (FWT)
Statement (informal):
Any competitive equilibrium allocation is Pareto efficient.

Sketch of logic:
• At equilibrium prices, each consumer chooses a utility-maximizing bundle subject to budget.
• Each firm chooses a profit-maximizing production plan.
• Feasibility and price-taking imply no reallocation can make someone better off without making someone else worse off.

=== Slide 09 ===
Assumptions Behind the FWT (Highlight)

Preferences & Technology:
• Locally non-satiated (or monotone) preferences.
• Convex preferences (often assumed).
• Convex, feasible production sets.
• No increasing returns.

Market Environment:
• Price-taking behavior.
• Complete markets (all goods/states can be traded).
• No externalities or public goods.
• Perfect information.
• Well-defined property rights.

Violations of these assumptions ⇒ potential market failures.

=== Slide 10 ===
When Competition Breaks: Sources of Imperfection
• Market power: monopoly, oligopoly.
• Externalities: actions impose costs/benefits on others.
• Public goods: non-rival, non-excludable.
• Information problems: adverse selection, moral hazard.
• Missing/Incomplete markets: some risks or goods not tradeable.

=== Slide 11 ===
Oligopoly at a Glance
• Few firms interact strategically ⇒ outcomes depend on conduct.

Models:
• Cournot (quantity competition): choose qi to maximize πi(qi, q−i).
• Bertrand (price competition): choose pi; with homogeneous goods and identical costs, equilibrium p ≈ marginal cost.
• Stackelberg (leadership): first-mover commits to q; follower best-responds.

Additional notes:
• Product differentiation, capacity constraints, and dynamics shift results away from perfect-competition benchmarks.

=== Slide 13 ===
What is Market Design?
Definition:
Market design is the engineering of rules and institutions to achieve good outcomes when the “natural” market fails or does not exist.

• Uses tools from game theory, mechanism design, and experiments.
• Applications:
  - auctions
  - matching markets
  - school choice
  - kidney exchange
  - spectrum allocation
  - ride-hailing
  - online platforms

=== Slide 14 ===
Design Objectives

Efficiency:
• High total surplus / Pareto improvements.
• Allocative efficiency with constraints.

Fairness:
• Stability, equity, diversity goals.
• Envy-freeness / no justified envy (context-specific).

Incentives:
• Strategy-proofness / truth-telling.
• Low manipulability; simple messages.

Practicality:
• Simplicity, transparency, legitimacy.
• Robustness to mistakes and missing data.

=== Slide 16 ===
Gale–Shapley Deferred Acceptance (High-Level)
1. Proposers (e.g., students) apply to top choice.
2. Receivers (e.g., schools) tentatively hold up to capacity per priority and reject the rest.
3. Rejected proposers apply to next choice; receivers update holds.
4. Repeat until no proposals are rejected.

Properties (with student-proposing):
• Produces a stable matching.
• Strategy-proof for proposers (truthful ranking is a dominant strategy).
• Proposer-optimal among all stable matchings.

=== Slide 17 ===
Designing a Matching Market: Checklist
• Define participants, capacities, and constraints (e.g., priorities, diversity, geography).
• Choose message space: rankings, scores, ties allowed?
• Select algorithm:
  - deferred acceptance
  - Top Trading Cycles (TTC)
  - variants (ties, couples, quotas)
• Decide objectives: stability vs. efficiency vs. fairness trade-offs.
• Build process:
  - timelines
  - information disclosure
  - simulations and pilots
  - user testing

=== Slide 18 ===
How to Design Markets? (General Playbook)
1. Diagnose: Identify failures (market power, externalities, information, missing markets, congestion).
2. Model: Map incentives & constraints. Define feasible outcomes and metrics.
3. Mechanism: Pick rules (pricing, priorities, auctions, matching, budgets).
4. Test: Simulate, experiment, stress-test for gaming and edge cases.
5. Implement: Keep it simple, transparent, enforceable.
6. Monitor & iterate: Use data to refine; guard against unintended consequences.

=== Slide 19 ===
Mechanism Design: Key Concepts
• A mechanism maps messages (bids, rankings) to outcomes and payments.
• Incentive compatibility: truthful messages are optimal.
• Individual rationality: participants prefer joining to opting out.
• Budget balance and feasibility constraints.

Examples:
• Vickrey auction (2nd price)
• VCG mechanisms
• Deferred acceptance
• Top Trading Cycles (TTC)

=== Slide 21 ===
Competitive Equilibrium (Formal Sketch)
Consumers i ∈ I:
maximize ui(xi) subject to p · xi ≤ p · ωi.

Firms f ∈ F:
choose yf ∈ Yf to maximize p · yf.

A competitive equilibrium is (p*, {x*i}, {y*f}) such that:
1. Utility/profit maximization at p*.
2. Market clearing:
   Σi x*i = Σi ωi + Σf y*f.

Under FWT assumptions ⇒ allocation {x*i} is Pareto efficient.
