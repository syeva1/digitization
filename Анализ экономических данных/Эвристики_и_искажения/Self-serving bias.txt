Self-serving bias
Created by Kozyrev Egor, Morozov Vasiliy,
Vaulina Maria, Zakharov Alexander
Definition
Self-serving bias is a phenomenon when people tend to attribute their achievements to their own skills but are likely to assign failures to some external/random factors.

Discovery and first research
The first scientific research into the self-serving bias belongs to Fritz Heider, an Austrian-American psychologist of the Gestalt school. In The Psychology of Interpersonal Relations(1958), he researched attribution (explanation of behaviour), which he classified as «internal» (attribution to one's own actions and qualities) and «external» (attribution to factors outside oneself).
Since then, researchers have explored the link between the self-serving bias and depression. Clinically depressed individuals exhibit this cognitive pattern in a different form, as they tend to attribute failures to their internal faults and characteristics while crediting successes to external factors and luck.
Further research was conducted by Bernard Weiner and Harold Kelley, creating a systematic theory of attribution. According to the former's ideas, main factors in forming attributions are one's ability, effort, the difficulty of task and luck.
In 1975 theory was expanded by Miller and Ross. They stated, that not only failures and victories can be reasons for person to be self-serving, but matches with persons expectations can also play a great role in it.

Video games
In competitive video games we often can see that developers are using this bias to keep players in the game. Due to this bias gamers think that their losses are a result of random factors or incompetent teammates, while their victories are achieved by skill.
