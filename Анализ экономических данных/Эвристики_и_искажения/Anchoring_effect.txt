Introduction

Anchoring effect is one the most common cognitive biases.
The essence of it is that people tend to exaggerate the
importance of previous information when making decision
even if this information is not connected to current situation.

One illustrative example

“Fathers” and development

First notion – by Slovic (1967), who studied descriptions of
preference reversals (as cited in Chapman and Johnson, 1999) [2]

Anchoring-and-adjustment heuristics by Tversky and
Kahneman (1974) in their pioneering work on judgment
under uncertainty [2]

Classical Experiment

Two randomly generated numbers between 10 and 65

Two groups and questions:
Is the share of African countries among the UN members
greater or less than the number you just got?
In your opinion, what proportion do African countries make up
among the UN members?

Average answers were 25% and 45% accordingly. [1]

Reasons of anchoring effect

There are two things which leads to anchoring.
They are associated with two different systems of our brain:

Anchoring effect as an attempt of adjustment

From this side this effect is a strategy for estimating
unknown values: we start with the number-anchor,
assess how small or large it is, and gradually adjust our
own assessment, mentally «departing from the anchor».
But we usually start to doubt at some moment
(Eldar Shafir and Tom Gilovich proved it)

Anchoring as a part of priming effect

System 1 understands sentences and tries to make them
true. Selective activation of suitable thoughts generates a
whole set of mistakes, making us prone to a belief in any
of our ideas (Thomas Mussweiler and Fritz Strack
demonstrated it)

Anchoring index

The anchoring effect can be measured, which is very rare
thing, and the results are all impressive.

Index will be 100% for those who submit to an anchor as
an assessment, and zero for those who are able to ignore
it.

55% – is a typical result
